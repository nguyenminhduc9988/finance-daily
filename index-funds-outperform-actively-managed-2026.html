7 Index Funds That Outperformed 95% of Actively Managed Funds in 2026

July 1, 2026 · 8 min read

Here's a number that should alarm anyone paying high mutual fund fees: over the past 15 years, 92.2% of large-cap actively managed funds underperformed their benchmark index, according to S&P Dow Jones Indices' SPIVA Scorecard. In some categories, that figure climbs above 95%.

The data is overwhelming and consistent — index funds don't just win sometimes, they win almost all the time, across almost every time horizon. In this article, we break down the 7 index funds that have delivered the strongest performance relative to active managers, and explain exactly why they keep winning.

Why Index Funds Consistently Win

The mathematics are deceptively simple. Active management charges higher fees — typically 0.5% to 1.5% annually vs. 0.03% for index funds. Over 30 years, that fee drag compounds into hundreds of thousands of dollars lost. But it's not just fees.

Active managers face structural disadvantages:

  • Trading costs: Higher turnover means more commissions, bid-ask spreads, and market impact costs
  • Tax inefficiency: Frequent trading generates short-term capital gains taxed at higher rates
  • Crowding risk: Many active managers end up hugging the benchmark anyway, delivering index-like returns with higher fees
  • Survivorship bias: The funds that survive are the lucky ones — failed funds get liquidated or merged, inflating reported active returns
Key Insight:

The SPIVA Scorecard tracks "survivorship-bias-free" returns, meaning it includes funds that were liquidated or merged. When you account for this, active management looks even worse. The real outperformance rate of active funds is closer to 2-5%, not the 8% often cited.

The 7 Index Funds That Dominate

1. Vanguard Total Stock Market Index (VTSMX / VTI)

VTI · Expense Ratio: 0.03%

This fund tracks the CRSP US Total Market Index, giving you exposure to essentially every publicly traded US company — over 4,000 holdings. It's the ultimate "set it and forget it" fund for US equity exposure.

+13.2% annualized (10yr)0.03% expense

VTI has beaten 94% of actively managed US large-cap funds over the past decade. Its broad diversification means you capture growth wherever it emerges — large-cap tech, mid-cap industrials, small-cap financials — without trying to predict which sector will lead.

2. Vanguard S&P 500 Index (VFINX / VOO)

VOO · Expense Ratio: 0.03%

The original index fund, tracking the S&P 500 — the 500 largest US companies by market cap. This is the benchmark that most active managers fail to beat, and owning it directly means you ARE the benchmark.

+12.8% annualized (10yr)0.03% expense

3. Vanguard Total International Stock (VXUS)

VXUS · Expense Ratio: 0.07%

International diversification matters more than most investors realize. VXUS holds over 8,000 stocks across developed and emerging markets, providing exposure to economies growing faster than the US.

+6.2% annualized (10yr)0.07% expense

While US stocks have dominated recently, the long-term case for international diversification remains strong. VXUS has beaten 88% of international active managers over 15 years.

4. Vanguard Total Bond Market (VBTLX / BND)

BND · Expense Ratio: 0.03%

BND tracks the Bloomberg US Aggregate Bond Index — essentially the total US investment-grade bond market. For the fixed-income portion of your portfolio, this fund delivers broad exposure at rock-bottom cost.

+1.4% annualized (10yr)0.03% expense

5. Fidelity Zero Large Cap Index (FNILX)

FNILX · Expense Ratio: 0.00%

Fidelity made waves by offering a truly zero-fee index fund. Tracking the Fidelity US Large Cap Index (essentially the S&P 500), FNILX proves that rock-bottom fees are sustainable in the index fund space.

+12.7% annualized (10yr)0.00% expense

If you're cost-sensitive, FNILX is hard to beat. A $100,000 investment over 30 years saves roughly $6,000 in fees compared to a typical 0.20% index fund — money that compounds in your favor.

6. Schwab U.S. Broad Market (SCHB)

SCHB · Expense Ratio: 0.03%

SCHB tracks the Dow Jones U.S. Broad Stock Market Index, covering roughly 2,500 US stocks. With Schwab's commission-free trading and competitive expense ratios, it's an excellent alternative to VTI.

+13.0% annualized (10yr)0.03% expense

7. iShares Core U.S. Aggregate Bond (AGG)

AGG · Expense Ratio: 0.03%

BlackRock's answer to BND, AGG tracks the same Bloomberg US Aggregate Bond Index. With over $100 billion in assets, it's one of the most liquid fixed-income ETFs available.

+1.3% annualized (10yr)0.03% expense

How to Build a Complete Index Fund Portfolio

You don't need all 7 funds. A simple three-fund portfolio covers all the bases:

The Three-Fund Portfolio

60% VTI (US Total Stock Market) — Growth engine

30% VXUS (International Stock) — Geographic diversification

10% BND (Total Bond Market) — Stability and income

This simple allocation has delivered competitive returns with lower volatility than most actively managed portfolios. Rebalance annually by selling winners and buying losers — it forces discipline and maintains your target allocation.

Common Misconceptions About Index Funds

"Index funds are boring." Yes, and that's the point. Boring, consistent returns beat exciting, volatile returns every time. The best investors are often the ones who check their portfolios least frequently.

"Active funds can beat the market in downturns." The data says otherwise. During the 2020 COVID crash, the 2022 bear market, and every major correction of the past 20 years, the majority of active funds still underperformed their benchmarks.

"You need a professional to manage your money." Unless you have a complex tax situation, illiquid assets, or unique circumstances, a low-cost index fund portfolio managed by a robo-advisor or DIY approach consistently outperforms professional management net of fees.

2026 Outlook: Index Funds Are Just Getting Started

The trend toward index investing continues to accelerate. In 2026, passive funds now hold over 50% of all US equity fund assets for the first time in history. As more money flows into index funds, market efficiency increases — making it even harder for active managers to find mispriced securities.

For individual investors, the implication is clear: the case for index funds in 2026 is stronger than ever. Lower fees, better tax efficiency, broader diversification, and consistent outperformance make them the rational choice for the vast majority of investors.
